1.1 Describing the Purpose of IT in Modern Business


The Business:

Information Technology in modern business provides the ability to communicate. Emails and online meetings are prime examples, of how a business can communicate within their team.

Technology provides business efficiency. For example, computer checks can avoid errors, which may come at a cost - these checks help support business growth. In addition to this, technology protects businesses from viruses and cyber attacks, which is important as there may be confidential documents and data. By having computers with passwords, a business can ensure none of its forthcoming projects will be copied by the competition. [1] This can also prevent hacking.

Information technology allows businesses to make better decisions. For example, Market research can now be done via online surveys (to get feedback), reviewing public opinion on social media, and through using Web analytics.
Business analytics is such a critical field. It enables companies to know their users' wants and needs.[2] Through analysing data, a business is able to continue growing by monitoring what works best.

Lastly, Information technology has given businesses the tools to stay organised. For instance, Kanban boards and Google Calendar have made it easier to plan.


The Customer:

Customers can receive support from multiple channels. Websites allow businesses to reach out to their customer. Information such as location, hours and contact info (which offers instant customer support) are all accessible. Benefitting both the customer and business.

Independently:

When working independently, it is important to plan and focus on your idea. You have to be able to create your own structure well. Trello is a collaboration tool that organises your projects into boards.[3]

In addition to this, stand-up meetings are effective for group projects as it offers structure and briefs everyone on the work that needs to be done for that day.

Lastly, adhering to deadlines is important, both in a team and independently. Deadlines provide a sense of motivation and achievement, which is key when creating a successful Digital Project.

1.4 Describe the Risks That Might Impact Digital Projects

Firstly, it is important to note the type of risks that may arise. For example, something that could go wrong during the project, and how to prevent that risk early on.

A huge risk that may impact digital projects to fail is lack of planning. A good digital project planning process will have the "Known/Known" risks built in. This means creating risk mitigation plans (Plan B) along with timing, and budget contingencies. [4] Planning provides structure, and being able to manage time more productively. Without a plan in place, you may find yourself focusing too much on the short-term goal, and vice versa - missing out on crucial ways to better your project.

Lastly, cost risk could potentially impact digital projects. This could be down to poor estimating accuracy and scope creep. [5] Businesses face competition, for instance, a competitor releases a new site with a great new feature that could draw your customers away.[6]
This reflects why planning and research is so crucial, as it can prevent a businesses loss.

1.7 Describe Legal Guidelines and Constraints that Impact Digital Projects

Websites are required to comply with legislation, and to be updated in accordance with the law. The GDPR regulations came into effect in May 2018. It is one of the most significant pieces of legislation improving the rights of individuals to understand how their personal data is being processed. [7] Below are a few examples of what websites must include to comply with GDPR:
Websites must be easy for users to withdraw their consent or opt out i.e. subscriptions.
Users must be notified of cookies that are being used to track their behaviour, the user would usually have to accept this.


A data breach process must be in place, as back-up. It is the responsibility of a business to prevent personal data from being compromised i.e. hacking. If data is to be breached, companies can face fines.
Websites must be accessible. If it isn't, you may be in breach of the Equality Act 2010. [8] Functions such as screen magnifiers and readers are an example of how to support people with disabilities.
